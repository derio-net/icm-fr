# Bias & Framing Analysis Framework

Not a left/right spectrum. Media bias is multidimensional. Use these 7 dimensions:

---

## 1. Frame Selection
What overarching story frame is used?
- "threat" / "opportunity" / "governance challenge" / "fait accompli"
- The frame determines which facts are salient and which are noise
- Same event can be framed as a crisis or a routine update

## 2. Salience Bias
What's front-and-center vs. buried?
- Headline angle vs. paragraph 15
- How much space each angle gets relative to its actual significance
- What gets a standalone story vs. a two-sentence mention in a roundup

## 3. Source Selection
Who's quoted? Who's absent?
- Institutional (officials, regulators) vs. affected parties (workers, users, communities)
- Named experts vs. anonymous sources
- Industry vs. civil society vs. government — which voices dominate
- Geographic and demographic diversity of quoted voices

## 4. Language & Tone
Word choices that imply stance:
- "crackdown" vs. "enforcement" vs. "policy update"
- "controversial" / "groundbreaking" / "alleged" / "so-called"
- Hedging ("may", "could") vs. declarative ("will", "is")
- Active vs. passive voice — who is doing what to whom

## 5. Causal Framing
Who or what is credited or blamed?
- Individual agency ("the CEO decided") vs. systemic cause ("market forces drove")
- Domestic vs. external causes
- Intent language — "deliberately" vs. "failed to" vs. "inadvertently"

## 6. Omission
What's not said that would change the picture?
- Historical context included or dropped
- Counterarguments presented or absent
- Which affected groups are mentioned vs. ignored
- Long-term consequences vs. immediate impact only

## 7. Proportion
How much space does each angle get?
- Horse-race coverage (who's winning politically) vs. policy substance
- Emotional human-interest angle vs. factual record
- Outlier position amplified vs. consensus buried

---

## How to Apply

For each article in the comparison:
1. Identify the **primary frame** (Frame Selection)
2. Note 1–2 **specific language choices** that reveal stance (Language & Tone)
3. Note **who's quoted** and who's conspicuously absent (Source Selection)
4. Note **what's missing** that would materially change the reader's understanding (Omission)

**Do NOT:**
- Assign left/right labels unless discussing explicit political spectrum coverage
- Assume bias from outlet reputation — derive it from the specific article in front of you
- Conflate factual error with framing choice (both matter, but differently — keep them separate)
- Treat framing as bad faith; framing is inherent to all journalism, not a conspiracy
